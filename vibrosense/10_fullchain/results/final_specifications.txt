## Known Limitations

1. **Accuracy still below target**: 2/4 (50%) vs 80% target. Normal
   misclassified as outer_race, ball misclassified as inner_race.
   However, the feature spread is now sufficient — the bottleneck has
   shifted from analog dynamic range to classifier training.

2. **Classifier not retrained on actual features**: The v4 classifier
   was trained on estimated peak ranges (0.928-0.993V) but actual
   simulated ranges are 0.918-1.040V. Retraining should improve
   accuracy — the features now have enough spread to distinguish classes.

3. **Peak detector is behavioral**: Currently uses SPICE B-sources.
   Transistor-level implementation available in Block 05.

4. **Synthetic vs real data**: Synthetic multi-tone bearing fault
   stimuli may not fully represent real CWRU accelerometer signatures.

## Honest Assessment

The peak detector envelope redesign is a significant step forward:

**What improved:**
- Envelope feature spread: 3-13 mV --> 8-121 mV (9x improvement)
- Classification accuracy: 25% --> 50% (doubled)
- Classifier stability: oscillating --> stable (100% on best case)
- Power consumption: 138 uW --> 51 uW (2.7x reduction)

**The bottleneck has shifted.** Previously, the analog dynamic range
was the fundamental limitation — the half-wave rectifier could not
produce meaningful DC shifts from small AC signals. Now, the peak
detector provides 96-121 mV spread on the most important channels
(ENV4, ENV5), which is well within the range needed for classification.

**The remaining accuracy gap is a classifier training problem**, not
an analog limitation. The v4 classifier was trained on estimated feature
ranges that don't match actual simulation. With retraining on real
simulated features, accuracy should improve to 3/4 or 4/4.

This validates the expert synthesis conclusion: peak detection is the
right approach for impulsive bearing faults. The analog feature
extraction chain now produces distinctive, well-separated features.
