## Known Limitations

1. **Envelope feature range too narrow**: After 3 fixes, envelope
   spread improved to 3-13 mV (up from 0.3-6.6 mV) but still
   insufficient for reliable classification. The half-wave rectifier
   envelope detector cannot produce large DC shifts from small AC
   signals near VCM.

2. **Classifier instability**: The retrained weights amplify tiny
   envelope differences into wildly swinging scores. The classifier
   flips between all 4 classes on ms timescales instead of locking
   onto one answer. All 4 classes now appear (improvement over before
   where only 2 classes appeared), but confidence is very low.

3. **Synthetic vs real data**: Synthetic multi-tone bearing fault
   stimuli do not sufficiently excite distinct per-channel spectral
   signatures. Real CWRU accelerometer data may produce more
   distinctive envelope patterns.

4. **Signal amplitude**: Even with V_SCALE=0.1 (5x increase), the
   BPF output swings are not large enough for the half-wave rectifier
   to generate meaningful DC envelope shifts above VCM.

## Honest Assessment

The VibroSense-1 full-chain simulation demonstrates:
- A working analog ML signal chain on a real 130nm CMOS PDK
- Total power of ~183 uW -- 5-50x better than MCU+FFT approaches
- Perfect classification when features are correctly pre-computed
- All analog blocks are stable and functional

Three targeted fixes (retrained weights, 5x amplitude boost,
per-channel BPF bias) produced measurable improvements:
- Envelope spread doubled on the most important channel (ENV4)
- Classifier now exercises all 4 output classes (was stuck on 2)
- Ball fault is now correctly classified

But accuracy remains at 25% (1/4). The fundamental bottleneck is the
envelope detector dynamic range. The half-wave rectifier approach
produces only millivolt DC shifts from the small AC signals present
in the BPF outputs. This is insufficient for the classifier to
reliably distinguish bearing fault types.

The path to higher accuracy requires:
1. Active rectifier or peak-detector envelope design (not half-wave)
2. Higher PGA gain (currently limited to 4x to prevent clipping)
3. Real CWRU accelerometer data as stimulus
4. Possibly: add temporal integration / averaging to classifier input

This is a legitimate first-silicon-equivalent result. The architecture
works in principle (100% on pre-computed vectors). The analog feature
extraction needs a fundamentally different envelope detector topology
to close the accuracy gap.
