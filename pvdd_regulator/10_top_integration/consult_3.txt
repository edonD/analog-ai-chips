CONSULT-3: Conditional Stability Analysis — Load-Dependent Phase Margin
=======================================================================
Date: 2026-04-03

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
-----------------
The sustained ~200mV pk-pk oscillation on the load transient plot is NOT
damped ringing — it is a limit-cycle oscillation indicating the loop has
zero or negative phase margin at the operating point. The root cause is
that the output pole (set by Rload || rds_pass and Cout) moves with load
current. At light load the output pole merges with the dominant pole,
creating ~180 degrees of phase shift well below UGB and destroying PM.
The loop is CONDITIONALLY STABLE: adequate (barely) at one load, unstable
at another.

=======================================================================
(1) EVIDENCE FROM THE PLOTS
=======================================================================

Load Transient (plot_load_transient.png):
  - 1→10→1 mA step, TT 27C
  - ~30+ cycles of sustained oscillation, 4.85V to 5.05V (~200mV pk-pk)
  - Oscillation frequency ~20 kHz (estimated from plot: ~30 cycles in 1.5ms)
  - NOT decaying — amplitude is constant throughout the entire window
  - This is a limit cycle, not transient ringing

Bode Plot (plot_bode.png):
  - Measured via PSRR injection method, TT 27C, 10 mA load (Rload=500 ohm)
  - DC loop gain = 70.8 dB
  - UGB ~ 142 kHz (extrapolated)
  - Phase at UGB ~ -150 degrees → PM ~ 30 degrees
  - Phase drops monotonically — no recovery above -180 before UGB

Key observation: 30 degrees PM gives damped ringing (Q ~ 2), but NOT
sustained oscillation. The sustained oscillation means PM is worse at a
DIFFERENT operating point — specifically at lighter load, where the loop
spends most of its time during the 1mA baseline portions of the test.

=======================================================================
(2) WHY PM VARIES WITH LOAD CURRENT — POLE MOVEMENT ANALYSIS
=======================================================================

The open-loop transfer function has these critical poles:

  p1 (dominant, Miller): set by EA Stage 1 output impedance and Miller-
    enhanced Cc=40pF. Location: ~1-5 Hz. Roughly load-independent.

  p_out (output): 1 / (2*pi * (Rload || rds_pass) * Cout_total)
    This pole MOVES with load current.
    Cout_total = 1uF + 200pF ≈ 1uF

  p2 (non-dominant, Miller-pushed): gm_cs * Cc / (Cd2 * Cgate)
    Roughly load-independent, ~500 kHz-1 MHz range.

  z1 (compensation zero): 1 / (2*pi * Rc * Cc) = 1/(2*pi*5k*40p) = 796 kHz

Output pole vs load:
  ┌──────────┬──────────┬────────────────┬────────────────────────────┐
  │ Iload    │ Rload    │ p_out          │ Comment                    │
  ├──────────┼──────────┼────────────────┼────────────────────────────┤
  │ 0 mA     │ open     │ ~0.1-1 Hz      │ rds_pass only, very high R │
  │ 1 mA     │ 5000 Ω   │ 32 Hz          │ MERGES WITH p1             │
  │ 5 mA     │ 1000 Ω   │ 159 Hz         │ Close to p1                │
  │ 10 mA    │ 500 Ω    │ 318 Hz         │ Bode plot condition        │
  │ 50 mA    │ 100 Ω    │ 1.59 kHz       │ Well separated from p1     │
  └──────────┴──────────┴────────────────┴────────────────────────────┘

At light load (1 mA): p_out ≈ 32 Hz is within a decade of p1 (~few Hz).
These two poles together contribute -180 degrees of phase shift by ~300 Hz.
The UGB is still in the kHz-to-tens-of-kHz range, so by UGB the phase has
already passed -180 degrees → PM is NEGATIVE.

At 10 mA: p_out ≈ 318 Hz, giving more separation from p1. Phase accumulates
more slowly. PM ≈ 30 degrees — marginal but positive.

At 50 mA: p_out ≈ 1.6 kHz, good separation. PM is best at heavy load,
probably 45-55 degrees.

=======================================================================
(3) QUANTITATIVE PM ESTIMATES VS LOAD
=======================================================================

Using the two-pole approximation (p1 + p_out dominate before UGB):

Phase at UGB ≈ -arctan(f_ugb/p1) - arctan(f_ugb/p_out) - arctan(f_ugb/p2) + arctan(f_ugb/z1)

The gain also changes with load because pass device gm varies:
  gm_pass ∝ sqrt(Id) for saturation: gm(10mA)/gm(1mA) ≈ sqrt(10) ≈ 3.2×
  → Loop gain drops ~10 dB at 1 mA vs 10 mA → UGB drops too

Estimated PM at each load (TT 27C):
  ┌──────────┬──────────┬──────────┬────────┬────────────────────────┐
  │ Iload    │ UGB est. │ PM est.  │ Status │ Transient behavior      │
  ├──────────┼──────────┼──────────┼────────┼────────────────────────┤
  │ 0 mA     │ ~10 kHz  │ -20 to   │ UNSTABLE│ Sustained oscillation  │
  │          │          │ -10 deg  │        │                         │
  │ 1 mA     │ ~30 kHz  │ -5 to    │ UNSTABLE│ Sustained oscillation  │
  │          │          │ +10 deg  │        │                         │
  │ 5 mA     │ ~80 kHz  │ 15-25°   │ MARGINAL│ Heavy ringing, slow    │
  │          │          │          │        │ decay                    │
  │ 10 mA    │ ~142 kHz │ ~30°     │ MARGINAL│ Ringing (Q~2-3)        │
  │ 50 mA    │ ~250 kHz │ 45-55°   │ OK-ish │ Some overshoot, settles │
  └──────────┴──────────┴──────────┴────────┴────────────────────────┘

CRITICAL: The load transient test steps between 1 mA and 10 mA. At 1 mA
baseline the loop is at or near instability. The "oscillation" seen in the
plot is the loop oscillating at the 1 mA operating point, NOT ringing from
the load step. This explains why it is SUSTAINED and does not decay.

=======================================================================
(4) IS THIS CONDITIONALLY STABLE?
=======================================================================

Strictly speaking, "conditional stability" means the Nyquist plot passes
through the third quadrant (phase < -180 degrees) at frequencies where
|T| > 1, but the phase recovers above -180 degrees before the gain drops
to 0 dB. In such a system, reducing the gain (e.g., by reducing load,
increasing beta, or component drift) can push the 0dB crossing into the
unstable phase region.

From the Bode at 10 mA:
  - Phase drops monotonically to -150 degrees at UGB
  - No visible phase recovery above -180 degrees
  - This does NOT look like classic conditional stability at 10 mA

However, the system IS conditionally stable WITH RESPECT TO LOAD:
  - At heavy load (50 mA): stable, PM > 45 degrees
  - At medium load (10 mA): marginally stable, PM ~ 30 degrees
  - At light load (1 mA): UNSTABLE, PM ~ 0 degrees or negative
  - During transitions: the operating point traverses unstable regions

This is a form of conditional stability where the "condition" is the DC
operating point (load current), not a frequency-domain gain margin issue.
The mechanism is the load-dependent output pole.

=======================================================================
(5) PHASE MARGIN REQUIREMENTS
=======================================================================

For different transient behaviors:

  PM > 76 degrees: Critical damping (Q = 0.5). No overshoot at all.
                    Monotonic settling. Required for precision references.

  PM > 65 degrees: Q < 0.5. At most one small overshoot cycle, then
                    monotonic. Acceptable for most LDOs.

  PM > 55 degrees: Q < 1. Moderate ringing, 2-3 cycles. Common minimum
                    spec for production LDOs.

  PM > 45 degrees: Q < 1.5. Visible ringing, 4-6 cycles. Marginal.

  PM ~ 30 degrees: Q ~ 2-3. Heavy ringing, 10-20 cycles. Unacceptable
                    for most applications.

  PM ~ 0 degrees:  Sustained oscillation. System failure.

Current state:
  - At 10 mA: PM ~ 30 degrees → Q ~ 2-3 (heavy ringing, confirmed by plot)
  - At 1 mA: PM ~ 0 degrees → sustained oscillation (confirmed by plot)
  - REQUIREMENT: PM > 65 degrees at ALL load conditions (0 to 50 mA)
  - This means we need ~35 degrees MORE phase margin at light load

=======================================================================
(6) ROOT CAUSE AND FIX DIRECTION
=======================================================================

The fundamental problem: the 1 uF output cap creates an output pole that
is load-dependent. At light load, this pole is too close to the dominant
pole, wiping out phase margin. The Miller compensation (Cc=40pF, Rc=5k)
was tuned for 10 mA where p_out ~ 318 Hz, but at 1 mA p_out drops to
32 Hz and the design falls apart.

This is a classic LDO stability problem. Solutions in order of effectiveness:

A. OUTPUT CAP ESR ZERO (most impactful for this topology)

   Add intentional ESR in series with Cout_ext:
     z_esr = 1 / (2*pi * Resr * Cout) = 1 / (2*pi * Resr * 1uF)

   To place the ESR zero near the output pole at light load (~32 Hz):
     Resr = 1/(2*pi * 32 * 1e-6) ≈ 5 kΩ — too high, causes DC drop

   Better: place z_esr near UGB to boost phase where it matters:
     For z_esr ~ 10-50 kHz: Resr = 1/(2*pi * 30kHz * 1uF) ≈ 5 Ω
     This adds 30-45 degrees of phase boost at UGB
     Trade-off: 5Ω × 10mA = 50mV output impedance bump

   Practical: Resr = 1-2 Ω gives z_esr at 80-160 kHz
     Still provides 10-20 degrees near UGB at 10 mA
     Less helpful at 1 mA where UGB is lower

B. FEEDFORWARD CAPACITOR (Cff) ACROSS R_TOP

   Creates a zero in the feedback path:
     z_ff = 1 / (2*pi * R_TOP * Cff) = 1 / (2*pi * 364k * Cff)

   For z_ff ~ 5-10 kHz: Cff ≈ 44-88 pF

   This boosts phase at frequencies above z_ff, providing 15-30 degrees
   additional PM. The zero location is load-independent — it helps at
   ALL load conditions equally.

   BUT: Cff also creates a high-frequency pole at:
     p_ff = 1 / (2*pi * (R_TOP||R_BOT) * Cff) ≈ 38 kHz (for 47pF)
   This limits the phase boost.

   Recommended: Cff = 22-47 pF. Start with 33 pF → z_ff ≈ 13 kHz.

C. REDUCE UGB (increase Cc further)

   Current Cc = 40 pF, UGB ~ 142 kHz at 10 mA.
   Increasing Cc to 100-200 pF would drop UGB to ~30-60 kHz.
   Lower UGB means less phase accumulation from p_out → more PM.

   But: This trades bandwidth for stability. Transient response gets
   slower. Undershoot = Iload_step * dt / Cout increases as bandwidth
   drops. Already at 40 pF the slew rate is only 0.575 V/us.

   Cc = 100 pF: UGB ~ 57 kHz, slew = 0.23 V/us. Transient response
   significantly degraded.

D. ADAPTIVE COMPENSATION (best but most complex)

   Use a load-sensing circuit to adjust compensation dynamically:
   - At light load: engage more compensation (larger effective Cc)
   - At heavy load: reduce compensation (higher bandwidth)

   This is common in commercial LDOs but complex for this design stage.

=======================================================================
(8) WHY THE BODE PLOT LOOKS "OK" BUT THE TRANSIENT DOESN'T
=======================================================================

The Bode was measured at 10 mA — the best-case stability point in the
1-10 mA test range. Even there PM is only ~30 degrees (unacceptable).

The load transient oscillates at the 1 mA operating point where PM is
near zero. The Bode at 10 mA simply doesn't capture the worst case.

This is the classic trap of LDO stability verification: measuring Bode
at one load and assuming it applies everywhere. LDO stability MUST be
verified at minimum load (including no-load) because that is where the
output pole is at its lowest frequency and PM is worst.

Additionally, during the load step transition itself, the operating point
sweeps through intermediate states. If PM goes negative at any point
during this sweep, the loop will oscillate. Even if the starting and
ending load conditions are individually stable, the transition can be
unstable — this is the "conditional stability during transition" failure
mode.

=======================================================================
(9) OSCILLATION FREQUENCY CHECK
=======================================================================

From the plot: f_osc ~ 20 kHz (30 cycles in ~1.5 ms)

For a system oscillating at PM = 0 degrees, the oscillation frequency
equals the frequency where phase(T) = -180 degrees and |T| ≥ 0 dB.

At 1 mA load:
  - p1 ~ 3 Hz (dominant)
  - p_out ~ 32 Hz (at Rload = 5 kΩ)
  - Both contribute -90 degrees each by ~300 Hz
  - Phase = -180 degrees at ~300 Hz... but gain is still very high there

  The oscillation occurs where |T| crosses 0 dB with phase ≤ -180 degrees.
  With 70 dB DC gain and -40 dB/decade slope (two poles), gain hits 0 dB at:
    f_0dB = sqrt(p1 * p_out) * 10^(70/40) ≈ sqrt(3 * 32) * 10^1.75
    ≈ 10 * 56 ≈ 560 Hz... this is lower than 20 kHz.

  The discrepancy suggests additional gain from the pass device gm stage
  and/or the Miller zero providing some gain boost. The exact oscillation
  frequency requires full SPICE verification.

  Alternative: the 20 kHz oscillation may be at the GAIN CROSSOVER with
  additional phase contributed by p2 (~500 kHz) — at 20 kHz, p2 contributes
  arctan(20k/500k) ≈ 2.3 degrees. Not significant.

  Most likely: the effective UGB at 1 mA is near 20 kHz, and the phase at
  20 kHz is exactly -180 degrees. This is consistent with ~10 dB lower loop
  gain at 1 mA (from reduced gm_pass) shifting UGB down from 142 kHz to
  ~30-50 kHz, with the closer output pole eroding phase.
